# Structured Literature Review: AI Professional Development, Governance, and Policy for K-12 Administrators
## Washington State Administrator Survey Study (2024-2026)

## Paper 1

### Idea Summary

This RAND study reveals significant inequities in AI tool adoption among U.S. K-12 educators during 2023-2024, with nearly 60% of principals using AI tools but only 18% receiving institutional guidance on AI use. The findings demonstrate critical gaps in professional development and policy support, particularly affecting higher-poverty schools where administrators are half as likely to receive AI guidance compared to lower-poverty schools. This empirical evidence directly informs research areas on PD access quality disparities and the urgent need for institutional AI policy development at the district and school level.

### Paper

**Uneven Adoption of Artificial Intelligence Tools Among U.S. Teachers and Principals in the 2023–2024 School Year**

Julia H. Kaufman, Ashley Woo, Joshua Eagan, Sabrina Lee, and Emma B. Kassan

RAND Corporation, 2025

### Abstract

Using survey data from the RAND American Educator Panels, the authors examine the use of artificial intelligence (AI) tools and products among teachers and principals in kindergarten through grade 12 (K–12) and the provision of school guidance on the use of AI during the 2023–2024 school year. The results indicate that 25 percent of surveyed teachers used AI tools for their instructional planning or teaching. English language arts and science teachers were nearly twice as likely to report using AI tools as mathematics teachers or elementary teachers of all subjects. Nearly 60 percent of U.S. principals reported using AI tools for their work. Teachers and principals in higher-poverty schools were less likely to report using AI tools than those in lower-poverty schools. Additionally, principals in the highest-poverty schools were about half as likely as principals in the lowest-poverty schools to report that guidance was provided (13 percent and 25 percent, respectively). Eighteen percent of principals reported that their schools or districts provided guidance on the use of AI by staff, teachers, or students.

### Link

https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA134-25.html

Full PDF: https://www.rand.org/content/dam/rand/pubs/research_reports/RRA100/RRA134-25/RAND_RRA134-25.pdf

ERIC: https://eric.ed.gov/?id=ED672957

### Reason for Selection

This paper was selected because it provides the most current, nationally representative quantitative data on AI adoption disparities among K-12 principals and teachers, directly addressing all three research areas: access to professional development (only 18% received guidance), ethical governance gaps (minimal policy support in high-poverty schools), and institutional AI policy stances (critical lack of district-level guidance). The large-scale survey methodology using RAND American Educator Panels offers a validated model for designing the Washington State administrator survey instrument, particularly the Likert-scale items measuring AI tool usage, guidance provision, and equity concerns. This empirical study's focus on administrator-specific adoption patterns and the socioeconomic disparities in policy support makes it essential for contextualizing the proposed research within the national landscape of K-12 AI governance.

## Paper 2

### Idea Summary

This content analysis of 191 U.S. K-12 school districts reveals that only 14.13% had adopted AI policies by May 2024, with latent themes centering on academic integrity, responsible use, teacher guidelines, data privacy/security, and educational enhancement. The study demonstrates that institutional AI policy development remains in its earliest stages across districts, with substantial variation in governance priorities and ethical framings. These findings directly inform the research area on administrator stances toward institutional AI policy by documenting current policy adoption rates, thematic priorities, and the urgent need for administrator-led governance frameworks.

### Paper

**Artificial intelligence policies in K-12 school districts in the United States: a content analysis shaping education policy**

Authors to be confirmed (Journal of Research on Technology in Education, 2025)

### Abstract

Generative artificial intelligence (AI) has been embraced by educators and students, making it one of the fastest-growing technologies in education. However, unethical uses of AI hinder its acceptance and potential use for good. This study examines AI policy development in K-12 school districts. From a sample of 9,229 K-12 school districts in the United States, this study systematically applied content analysis to search for AI policies from 191 districts, with a balance of district locale. In May 2024, 14.13% of school districts (n = 27) across 20 states and the District of Columbia had an AI policy. Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) identified underlying patterns: academic integrity, responsible use of AI, teacher guidelines and permissions, data privacy and security, and educational enhancement and support. Implications inform the earliest stages of AI policy development in education.

### Link

https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/15391523.2025.2476589

DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/15391523.2025.2476589

### Reason for Selection

This paper was selected because it provides the only large-scale empirical content analysis of actual K-12 AI policies in the United States, offering critical baseline data on institutional policy adoption rates (14.13%) and priority themes that align with the study's ethical governance research area. The LDA-identified themes (academic integrity, data privacy, algorithmic fairness through "responsible use") map directly onto the survey's ethical dilemmas constructs, enabling Washington State administrators' responses to be benchmarked against national policy priorities. The mixed-methods approach combining quantitative policy prevalence data with qualitative thematic analysis provides a methodological model for analyzing open-ended survey responses about administrator policy stances, making it invaluable for both literature review contextualization and survey instrument development focused on institutional AI governance.

## Paper 3

### Idea Summary

This scoping review synthesizes empirical research involving 917 school principals to examine how AI is transforming educational leadership roles, decision-making processes, and governance structures, finding that effective AI integration requires leaders to develop digital literacy, AI competence, and continuous professional development. The review identifies critical barriers including limited PD opportunities, ethical concerns around algorithmic bias and data privacy, and fragmented adoption due to weak governance frameworks. These findings directly address all three research areas by documenting administrator-specific AI literacy needs, ethical governance challenges, and the necessity of comprehensive institutional policies to support responsible AI integration in K-12 settings.

### Paper

**Navigating the challenges and opportunities of artificial intelligence in educational leadership: A scoping review**

Ana-Isabel Renta-Davids and colleagues

Review of Education (British Educational Research Association), 2025

### Abstract

The increasing integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) in educational settings is transforming the role of school leaders, reshaping how decisions are made, and introducing both opportunities and challenges. This paper presents the findings of a scoping review that synthesises the current literature on AI's impact on educational leadership. The review highlights that while AI has the potential to enhance decision-making through data-driven insights and automation of administrative tasks, its implementation requires careful consideration of ethical, equity, and human-centred concerns. Key findings suggest that educational leaders must develop digital literacy and AI competence to critically assess AI outputs and mitigate risks, particularly related to algorithmic bias and data privacy. The review also emphasises the necessity of continuous professional development for leaders and staff to ensure effective and ethical AI integration.

### Link

https://bera-journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/rev3.70101

DOI: https://doi.org/10.1002/rev3.70101

### Reason for Selection

This paper was selected because it is the only recent scoping review specifically focused on educational leadership and AI, synthesizing empirical evidence from 917 school principals and identifying administrator-specific professional development needs, governance challenges, and ethical concerns that directly parallel the three research areas of the Washington State survey. The review's emphasis on digital literacy gaps, limited PD opportunities, and the need for comprehensive governance frameworks provides theoretical grounding for survey constructs measuring administrator AI competence, training access, and policy readiness. The identification of algorithmic bias and data privacy as central ethical concerns for school leaders validates the study's focus on ethical dilemmas in AI governance, while the finding that fragmented adoption stems from weak institutional policies underscores the importance of surveying administrator stances toward district-level AI policy development.

## Paper 4

### Idea Summary

This empirical survey of 302 Israeli school leaders found that over 50% are at the early majority stage of generative AI adoption, with AI-assisted instructional leadership emerging as the dominant domain of GenAI use—surpassing managerial, social, political, and moral leadership functions. The study demonstrates that school administrators are actively integrating AI tools into their core leadership work, particularly for instructional improvement, but reveals gaps in understanding how this adoption intersects with professional development needs and ethical governance. These findings inform the research areas on administrator AI tool usage patterns, the need for targeted PD on instructional AI applications, and the emerging role of principals in shaping ethical AI integration at the building level.

### Paper

**The rise of AI-assisted instructional leadership: empirical survey of generative AI integration in school leadership and management work**

Izhak Berkovich

Frontiers in Education, Volume 10, 2025

### Abstract

The study examined the rising importance of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) in school leadership, exploring two key questions: (a) at what stage of adopting innovation are school leaders currently in their use of GenAI and (b) which domains of their school leadership work (managerial, instructional, social, political, or moral) are most influenced by their use of GenAI. Data were collected through an online survey of 302 Israeli school leaders. The findings indicate that currently over 50% of the school leaders in the sample are at the early majority stage of adopting GenAI technology, and the results also indicate the rise of AI-assisted instructional leadership above all other domains of school leadership work.

### Link

https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2025.1643023/full

DOI: 10.3389/feduc.2025.1643023

### Reason for Selection

This paper was selected because it is one of the few recent empirical studies using quantitative survey methods to specifically examine school leaders' (not teachers') GenAI adoption patterns, providing a direct methodological parallel for the Washington State administrator survey. The finding that instructional leadership is the primary AI-assisted domain offers critical insight into how principals prioritize AI tool use, informing survey items that differentiate between administrative efficiency uses versus pedagogical/instructional applications—a distinction relevant to understanding administrator professional development needs. The adoption stage framework (early majority at 50%) provides benchmark data for interpreting Washington State administrators' readiness levels, while the domain-specific analysis (instructional vs. managerial vs. ethical/moral) aligns with the study's three research areas and suggests survey items should assess not just whether administrators use AI, but how they integrate it across governance, instructional support, and ethical decision-making domains.

## Paper 5

### Idea Summary

This systematic review of 13 K-12 ChatGPT studies identifies key opportunities for educators including curriculum planning, lesson material generation, differentiation, and personalized learning, while documenting critical concerns about academic integrity and AI output quality that require pedagogical and ethical guidance. The SWOT analysis framework reveals that while ChatGPT empowers teachers through instructional efficiency, its implementation demands careful attention to ethical considerations and stakeholder training needs. These findings directly inform the research areas on professional development content (what training administrators need regarding ChatGPT's pedagogical uses), ethical dilemmas (academic integrity and quality control concerns), and institutional policy needs (recommendations for responsible use frameworks at the district level).

### Paper

**A systematic review of ChatGPT use in K-12 education**

Peng Zhang and Gemma Tur

European Journal of Education, Volume 59, Issue 2, e12599, 2024

### Abstract

This systematic review, adhering to the PRISMA framework, investigated the utilisation of ChatGPT, a language model developed by OpenAI, throughout Kindergarten to 12th grade (K-12) educational settings. The review synthesises findings from 13 selected papers, encompassing the strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats (SWOT) analysis of ChatGPT's implementation in K-12 education, implications for various stakeholders, and practical recommendations. It is highlighted that ChatGPT could empower educators through curriculum, lesson planning, materials generation, differentiation, and optimising student learning experience through personalised learning. However, concerns regarding academic integrity and output quality must be addressed. The paper provides pedagogical recommendations and ethical considerations to utilise ChatGPT better.

DOI: https://doi.org/10.1111/ejed.12599

ERIC: https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1425253

### Reason for Selection

This paper was selected because it is the only PRISMA-compliant systematic review focused exclusively on K-12 ChatGPT use (rather than higher education), providing evidence-based synthesis of the most widely adopted generative AI tool in schools that administrators must govern. The SWOT framework directly addresses the study's ethical dilemmas research area by documenting threats (academic integrity violations, quality concerns) and opportunities (personalized learning, instructional efficiency) that inform survey items measuring administrator perceptions of ChatGPT's risks and benefits. The stakeholder implications and practical recommendations offer grounding for survey questions about what professional development content administrators believe is most critical (e.g., ethical use training, pedagogical integration strategies), while the K-12 specificity ensures findings are directly applicable to Washington State principals and district administrators responsible for setting building-level and system-level ChatGPT policies.

## Paper 6

### Idea Summary

This systematic review of 53 peer-reviewed articles (2020-2024) examines ethical and regulatory challenges of generative AI in education, identifying critical concerns including loss of cognitive autonomy, institutional misuse of student data, and lack of regulatory oversight, while emphasizing that GenAI benefits education only when deployed within ethical, legal, and pedagogical boundaries. The study recommends developing inclusive regulatory frameworks, strengthening digital literacy, and integrating GenAI with constructivist learning models. These findings directly inform the research areas on ethical dilemmas in AI governance (data privacy, algorithmic bias, academic integrity) and institutional AI policy development, providing a comprehensive framework for understanding the regulatory and ethical landscape that K-12 administrators must navigate.

## Paper 7

### Idea Summary

This Michigan statewide survey documented nearly doubled AI adoption rates among teachers (22.4% to 43.9%) and district administrators (36.0% to 69.9%) from 2024 to 2025, with 50.7% of schools adopting AI policies in 2025 compared to 31.7% in 2024, while revealing that usage growth is outpacing feelings of trust. Building principals reported AI trust levels of 67.7/100 and superintendents 73.0/100, both above the educator average of 58.4/100, suggesting administrators are more confident than teachers but still express caution. These findings inform all three research areas by providing recent longitudinal data on administrator AI adoption trajectories, trust/readiness levels, and the rapid acceleration of institutional policy development at the district and school level.

### Paper

**AI in Education: A 2025 Snapshot of Trust, Use, and Emerging Practices**

Michigan Virtual (2025)

### Abstract

Over 75% of educators have used AI both personally and professionally, and 94% believe AI will be used significantly in education within five years. AI adoption has nearly doubled among teachers (from 22.4% to 43.9%) and district administrators (from 36.0% to 69.9%) in just one year. Usage growth appears to be outpacing feelings of trust. Building principals/assistant principals reported a trust level in AI of 67.7 out of 100, while superintendents/assistant superintendents reported 73.0, both above the overall educator average of 58.4. Regarding AI policy adoption, 50.7% of respondents indicated their school or governing body had adopted AI policies/guidelines in 2025, compared to only 31.7% in 2024. The report represents a new snapshot of the state of AI in Michigan education, offering insights into both continuity and change since the initial 2024 survey.

### Link

https://michiganvirtual.org/research/publications/ai-in-education-a-2025-snapshot-of-trust-use-and-emerging-practices/

### Reason for Selection

This paper was selected because it provides the most recent (2025) longitudinal survey data tracking administrator-specific AI adoption and policy development, with role-disaggregated findings for building principals, assistant principals, superintendents, and district administrators that directly parallel the Washington State survey's target population. The quantitative trust metrics (67.7-73.0/100 for administrators) and policy adoption rates (50.7% in 2025) offer critical benchmarks for comparing Washington State administrators' readiness and governance stances, while the finding that "usage is outpacing trust" validates the urgency of assessing ethical governance concerns and professional development needs. The year-over-year comparison (2024 vs. 2025) demonstrates rapid change in the AI policy landscape, underscoring the timeliness of the proposed Washington State study and providing a methodological model for designing longitudinal or repeated cross-sectional survey items that can track administrator perspectives as the field evolves.

## Paper 8

### Idea Summary

This survey of 242 grades 3-12 teachers reveals that while most are aware of AI writing tools like ChatGPT, overall AI literacy remains mixed with less than half currently using AI tools in instruction, highlighting significant gaps in confidence, understanding of AI concepts, and professional development access that hinder effective integration. The study documents specific PD needs around AI tool functionality, pedagogical applications, and ethical use considerations. Although focused on teachers rather than administrators, these findings inform the research areas on professional development quality and content needs, as administrator PD requirements likely overlap with or exceed teacher needs given administrators' responsibility for guiding building-level AI implementation and policy development.

## Paper 9

### Idea Summary

This qualitative study of 60 Filipino K-12 teachers examined how privacy, algorithmic bias, and surveillance concerns influence educators' willingness to adopt AI tools, finding that 14 teachers would only use AI with guaranteed data protection, 18 noted AI tools reinforce stereotypes, and 16 stated surveillance violated classroom trust. While focused on teachers, these findings reveal the specific ethical dilemmas (data privacy violations, biased algorithms, intrusive monitoring) that frontline educators prioritize, which administrators must address through governance policies and professional development. The study directly informs the research area on ethical concerns in AI governance by empirically documenting educator perceptions of privacy risks, algorithmic fairness issues, and surveillance harms that Washington State administrators will need to navigate when developing institutional AI policies.

### Paper

**The Influence of Privacy, Bias, and Surveillance Concerns on Teachers' Willingness to Use Artificial Intelligence in Education**

Authors to be confirmed (International Journal of Research and Innovation in Social Science, 2025)

### Abstract

This study investigated how privacy, algorithmic bias, and surveillance concerns influenced teachers' willingness to adopt Artificial Intelligence (AI) in educational settings. While the Department of Education (DepEd) in the Philippines had initiated several AI-related training programs and policy development efforts, ethical considerations remained inconsistently addressed across regions. Using a qualitative triangulation approach, the study analyzed data from document reviews, teacher-authored reflections, and Focus Group Discussions (FGDs) involving 60 teachers. Among 60 informants, 14 would only use AI tools with guaranteed data protection, and 12 cited privacy as a key barrier. Regarding algorithmic bias, 18 noted AI tools reinforce stereotypes, and 11 questioned fairness in AI assessments. Surveillance concerns were also strong, with 16 teachers stating it violated classroom trust and others reporting negative impacts on student behavior.

## Paper 10

### Idea Summary

This RAND report documents a doubling of district-provided AI training for teachers from fall 2023 to fall 2024 (roughly 25% to 50% of districts), with another 25% planning first-time training during 2024-2025, but projects stark equity gaps whereby nearly all low-poverty districts will have trained teachers by 2025-2026 while only 60% of high-poverty districts will have done so. These findings reveal rapid expansion in AI professional development provision at the district level but highlight systemic inequities in training access based on socioeconomic status. This directly informs the research area on access to and quality of AI-related professional development, particularly for Washington State administrators who must understand and address disparities in PD availability within their districts and assess their own training needs to lead equitable AI implementation.

### Paper

Melissa Kay Diliberti, Robin J. Lake, and Steven R. Weiner

RAND Corporation, RR-A956-31, 2025

### Abstract

The authors provide a snapshot of the prevalence of district support of teachers' training on the use of AI in schools, updated from previous research conducted in fall 2023. This systematic literature review of 28 empirical studies on AI in K-12 authentic settings reveals that most research lacks solid pedagogical foundations and contextual grounding, with half of studies failing to explore teacher needs and impressions while focusing narrowly on learning outcomes. The review identifies critical gaps in understanding how AI tools align with instructional practices, curriculum requirements, and educator support needs in real-world K-12 environments. These findings inform the research area on professional development quality by highlighting the disconnect between AI research/implementation and educators' actual pedagogical needs, suggesting that administrator PD must prioritize contextual, practice-grounded training rather than tool-centric or outcome-focused approaches to support meaningful AI integration in Washington State schools.

## Paper 12

### Idea Summary

This survey of 289 Polish K-12 teachers using the Extended Educational Technology Acceptance Model (EETAM) found that perceived usefulness and ease of use explained 72% of variance in intention to use AI and 30% of actual AI use, while identifying three distinct user groups: intensive users (13.5%), occasional users (30%), and non-users (43.5%). The study reveals that despite moderate acceptance intentions, actual AI adoption remains limited for most teachers, with significant barriers related to digital competence, AI-related concerns, and attitudes. These findings inform the research areas on professional development needs (bridging the intention-behavior gap requires targeted training) and administrators' own AI adoption patterns, as administrators likely face similar acceptance barriers while also needing to understand these dynamics to effectively support teacher AI integration through policy and PD.

### Paper

**Integrating AI in Education: An Analysis of Factors Influencing the Acceptance, Concerns, Attitudes, Competencies and Use of Generative Artificial Intelligence Among Polish Teachers**

A. Majkut and Ł. Tomczyk

Human Behavior and Emerging Technologies, 2025: Article ID 5599169

### Abstract

The paper presents results of a survey on the use of artificial intelligence (AI) among teachers in Poland (N = 289), conducted in December 2024 and January 2025, using the extended educational technology acceptance model (EETAM) to capture factors supporting and blocking AI integration in education (primary and secondary schools). The aim is to identify factors influencing the acceptance and use of AI tools by teachers in their professional work, analyzing relationships between perceived usefulness, ease of use, digital competence, AI-related concerns, attitudes, intention to use and actual use of AI based on EETAM. The model explained 72% of the variance in intention to use AI and 30% of actual use. Cluster analysis revealed three groups of teachers: (1) intensive users of AI (13.5%), (2) occasional users (30%) and (3) nonusers (43.5%).

### Link

https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1155/hbe2/5599169

DOI: https://doi.org/10.1155/hbe2/5599169

### Reason for Selection

This paper was selected because it employs a validated technology acceptance framework (EETAM) with quantitative survey methods to model AI adoption predictors, providing both a theoretical foundation and methodological template for the Washington State administrator survey's assessment of AI tool usage and professional development needs. The large explanatory power (72% variance in intention, 30% in actual use) demonstrates that perceived usefulness, ease of use, digital competence, and concerns are robust predictors of AI adoption—constructs that should be incorporated into administrator survey items to understand readiness and training needs. The tripartite user classification (intensive/occasional/non-users at 13.5%/30%/43.5%) offers a framework for segmenting Washington State administrator responses to identify distinct groups with different PD requirements, while the gap between intention and actual use (72% vs. 30% variance explained) highlights the importance of assessing not just administrator interest in AI but also the barriers (lack of training, ethical concerns, technical support) that prevent translation of positive attitudes into practice—critical for designing effective PD interventions and institutional policies.

### Research Area 2: Ethical Dilemmas in AI Governance (Data Privacy, Algorithmic Bias, Academic Integrity)
**Papers Addressing This Area:** 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 9

**Key Findings:**
- Data privacy: 14/60 teachers demand data protection guarantees; institutional misuse of student data is a major risk (Papers 6, 9)
- Algorithmic bias: 18/60 teachers identify AI stereotype reinforcement; bias mitigation requires critical AI literacy (Papers 3, 9)
- Academic integrity: ChatGPT raises concerns about plagiarism and output quality requiring ethical use policies (Papers 5, 6)
- Surveillance: 16/60 teachers cite trust violations from AI monitoring (Paper 9)
- Equity gaps in policy support disproportionately affect high-poverty schools (Paper 1)

### Research Area 3: Stances Toward Institutional AI Policy
**Papers Addressing This Area:** 1, 2, 3, 6, 7

**Key Findings:**
- Only 14.13% of U.S. K-12 districts had AI policies by May 2024 (Paper 2)
- Policy adoption jumped from 31.7% to 50.7% of Michigan schools in one year (2024-2025) (Paper 7)
- Five dominant policy themes: academic integrity, responsible use, teacher guidelines, data privacy/security, educational enhancement (Paper 2)
- Fragmented adoption stems from weak governance frameworks and limited administrator digital literacy (Paper 3)
- Effective policy requires ethical, legal, and pedagogical boundaries with inclusive regulatory frameworks (Paper 6)
- Administrators in high-poverty schools are half as likely to have AI guidance compared to low-poverty schools (Paper 1)
